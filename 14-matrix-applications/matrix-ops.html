14.2. Matrix Operations#

Matrices (and more generally, arrays) have a wide variety of operations that are defined between multiple matrices and between matrices, vectors, and scalars. We introduce the most important of these operations below.

14.2.1. Matrix Addition and Subtraction#

One of the most simple operations to define is addition between two matrices of the same dimensions. For \(m \times n\) matrices \(\mathbf{X}\) and \(\mathbf{Y}\), the sum \( \mathbf{X} + \mathbf{Y}\) is defined as the elementwise sum:

\[\begin{equation*} \mathbf{X} + \mathbf{Y} = \begin{bmatrix} x_{0,0} + y_{0,0} & x_{0,1} + y_{0,1} & \ldots & x_{0,n-1} + y_{0,n-1} \\ x_{1,0} + y_{1,0} & x_{1,1} + y_{1,1} & \ldots & x_{1,n-1} + y_{0,n-1} \\ \vdots & \vdots & \ldots & \vdots \\ x_{m-1,0} + y_{m-1,0} & x_{m-1,1} + y_{m-1,1} & \ldots & x_{m-1,n-1} + y_{m-1,n-1} \\ \end{bmatrix}. \end{equation*}\]

Matrix addition in NumPy uses the usual + operator:

X = np.array([[1, 2],
              [3, 4]
             ])
Y = np.array([[1, -1],
              [1, -1]
             ])
print(X+Y)

Similarly, matrix subtraction is denoted \(\mathbf{X} - \mathbf{Y}\) and defined by elementwise subtraction,

\[\begin{equation*} \mathbf{X} - \mathbf{Y} = \begin{bmatrix} x_{0,0} - y_{0,0} & x_{0,1} - y_{0,1} & \ldots & x_{0,n-1} - y_{0,n-1} \\ x_{1,0} - y_{1,0} & x_{1,1} - y_{1,1} & \ldots & x_{1,n-1} - y_{0,n-1} \\ \vdots & \vdots & \ldots & \vdots \\ x_{m-1,0} - y_{m-1,0} & x_{m-1,1} - y_{m-1,1} & \ldots & x_{m-1,n-1} - y_{m-1,n-1} \\ \end{bmatrix}. \end{equation*}\]

Matrix subtraction with NumPy arrays uses the usual - operator:

print(X-Y)

14.2.1.1. Properties of Matrix Addition#

Because matrix addition is component-wise scalar addition, it inherits many of its properties from scalar addition:

  • Commutative \(\mathbf{A} + \mathbf{B} = \mathbf{B} + \mathbf{A}\)

  • Associative: \(( \mathbf{A} + \mathbf{B}) + \mathbf{C} = \mathbf{A} + (\mathbf{B} + \mathbf{C})\)

  • Identity: The additive identity is the zero matrix: \(\mathbf{A} + \mathbf{0} = \mathbf{0}+ \mathbf{A}= \mathbf{A}\).

NumPy Only: Matrix-Scalar Addition and Subtraction

Addition and subtraction between a matrix and a scalar is not usually defined mathematically. However, NumPy will broadcast scalar addition or subtraction across all of the elements of a matrix. Thus, if \(c\) is a scalar, then \(\mathbf{X} +c\) is defined as

\[\begin{equation*} \mathbf{X} + c = \begin{bmatrix} x_{0,0} + c & x_{0,1} + c & \ldots & x_{0,n-1} + c \\ x_{1,0} + c & x_{1,1} + c & \ldots & x_{1,n-1} + c \\ \vdots & \vdots & \ldots & \vdots \\ x_{m-1,0} + c & x_{m-1,1} + c & \ldots & x_{m-1,n-1} + c \\ \end{bmatrix}. \end{equation*}\]

Subtraction is defined similarly. Examples are below:

X = np.array([[1, 2],
              [3, 4]
             ])
print(X+10)
print()
print(X-1)

14.2.2. Scalar-Matrix Multiplication#

A matrix may be left-multiplied or right-multiplied by a scalar with the same result: each element in the matrix is multiplied by the scalar. We can consider this to be broadcasting the multiplication across the elements of the array. Thus,

\[\begin{equation*} c \mathbf{X} = \mathbf{X} c = \begin{bmatrix} c x_{0,0} & c x_{0,1} & \ldots & c x_{0,n-1} \\ c x_{1,0} & c x_{1,1} & \ldots & c x_{1,n-1} \\ \vdots & \vdots & \ldots & \vdots \\ c x_{m-1,0} & c x_{m-1,1} & \ldots & c x_{m-1,n-1} \\ \end{bmatrix}. \end{equation*}\]

14.2.2.1. Properties of Scalar Multiplication#

Since scalar multiplication is component-wise multiplication by a scalar, it inherits the properties below from normal multiplication of real values.

If \(\mathbf{X}\) is a vector and \(c\) is a real scalar, then these properties hold:

  • Commutative: \(c\mathbf{X} = \mathbf{X} c.\) It does not matter whether the multiplying scalar is on the right or left of the vector.

  • Associative: If \(b\) and \(c\) are scalars, then \((bc) \mathbf{X} = b (c \mathbf{X})\). If multiplying by two scalars, we will get the same result if we do the scalar-scalar multiplication first or the scalar-matrix multiplication first.

  • Distributive over scalar addition: \((b+c) \mathbf{X} = b \mathbf{X} + c \mathbf{X}\) and \(\mathbf{X} (b+c) = \mathbf{X}b + \mathbf{X}c \).

  • Distributive over matrix addition: \(c ( \mathbf{X} +\mathbf{Y}) =c \mathbf{X} + c \mathbf{Y}\).

14.2.3. Dot Product as Matrix Multiplication#

We will build up our definition of matrix multiplication by starting with dot product of two \(n\)-vectors. Recall our definition of the dot product from Chapter 12:

DEFINITION

dot product, inner product

Given \(n\)-vectors \(\mathbf{x}\) and \(\mathbf{y}\), the dot product or inner product is denoted \(\mathbf{x} \cdot \mathbf{y}\) or \(\mathbf{x}^T \mathbf{y}\) and is the scalar value given by multiplying corresponding components and summing them up:

\[\begin{equation*} \mathbf{x} \cdot \mathbf{y} = \sum_{i=0}^{n-1} x_i y_i. \end{equation*}\]

Note that \(n\)-vectors are generally treated as \(n \times 1\) “tall” matrices. For instance, the vector \(\mathbf{x}\) can be written as

\[\begin{equation*} \mathbf{x} = \begin{bmatrix} x_0 \\ x_1 \\ \vdots \\ x_{n-1} \\ \end{bmatrix}. \end{equation*}\]

Note also that the dot product of \(\mathbf{x}\) and \(\mathbf{y}\) can be written in two forms. Until now, we have used the form \(\mathbf{x} \cdot \mathbf{y}\), but the dot product can also be written as \(\mathbf{x}^T \mathbf{y}\), which looks like this:

(14.2)#\[\begin{split}\mathbf{x}^T \mathbf{y} = \begin{bmatrix} x_0 & x_1 & \ldots &x_{n-1} \ \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} y_0 \\ y_1 \\ \vdots \\ y_{n-1} \\ \end{bmatrix} \\ = x_0 y_0 + x_1 y_1 + \ldots + x_{n-1} y_{n-1},\end{split}\]

where the last line comes from the definition of the dot product.

The vector \(\mathbf{x}^T\) is called a row vector, while \(\mathbf{y}\) is a column vector. We use (14.2) to define multiplication between a row vector and a column vector.

14.2.5. Matrix Norm#

The norm of an \(m \times n\) matrix \(\mathbf{A}\) is $\( \left\| \mathbf{A} \right\| = \sqrt{ \sum_{i=0}^{m-1}\sum_{j=0}^{n-1} A_{ij}^{2} } \)$

Matrix norm satisifies several properties:

  • Triangle Inequality \(\left\| \mathbf{A} + \mathbf{B} \right\| \le \left\| \mathbf{A} \right\| + \left\| \mathbf{B} \right\| \)

  • Norm of Transposed Matrix \(\left\| \mathbf{A}^T \right\| = \left\| \mathbf{A} \right\|\)

  • Relation to Vector Norms If \(\mathbf{a}_0, \mathbf{a}_1, \ldots, \mathbf{a}_{n-1}\) are the columns of a matrix \(\mathbf{A}\), then \(\left\| \mathbf{A} \right\|^2 = \left\| \mathbf{a}_0 \right\|^2 + \left\| \mathbf{a}_1 \right\| ^2+ \ldots + \left\| \mathbf{a}_{n-1} \right\|^2\)

We can use the matrix norm to define distance between two matrices \(\mathbf{A}\) and \(\mathbf{B}\) as \(\left\| \mathbf{A} - \mathbf{B} \right\|\). Small distances imply the two matrices are similar

14.2.7. Matrices and Linear Equations#

We can use matrices and vectors to concisely express systems of linear equations and to efficiently solve them

We begin by defining vector-vector functions

The notation \(\mathbf{f}: \mathbb{R}^n \Rightarrow \mathbb{R}^m\) means that \(\mathbf{f}\) is a function that takes a \(n\)-vector as input and produces an \(m\)-vector as output

(I will use bold letters for the function \(f\) to indicate that it is a vector function)

One special case is the matrix-vector multiplication function: $\( \mathbf{f}(\mathbf{x}) = \mathbf{Ax}, \)\( where \)\mathbf{A}\( is a \)m \times n$ matrix

As usual, the vector-vector function \(\mathbf{f}(\mathbf{x})\) is linear if it satisfies the superposition property: $\( \mathbf{f} \left( \alpha \mathbf{x} + \beta \mathbf{y} \right) = \alpha \mathbf{f} \left( \mathbf{x}\right) + \beta\mathbf{f} \left( \mathbf{y} \right) \)$

The matrix-vector multiplication function \(\mathbf{f}(\mathbf{x}) = \mathbf{Ax}\) is linear:

(14.3)#\[\begin{align} \mathbf{f} \left( \alpha \mathbf{x} + \beta \mathbf{y} \right) &=\mathbf{A} \left( \alpha \mathbf{x} + \beta \mathbf{y} \right)\\ &= \mathbf{A} \left( \alpha \mathbf{x}\right) + \mathbf{A} \left( \beta \mathbf{y} \right) \\ &= \alpha \left( \mathbf{A} \mathbf{x}\right) + \beta \left( \mathbf{A} \mathbf{y} \right) \\ &= \mathbf{f} \left( \alpha \mathbf{x}\right) + \mathbf{f} \left( \beta \mathbf{y} \right) \end{align}\]

In fact, if \(\mathbf{f}: \mathbb{R}^n \Rightarrow \mathbb{R}^m\), then \(\mathbf{f}\) can be written as \(\mathbf{f}(\mathbf{x}) = \mathbf{Ax}\), where \(\mathbf{A}\) is unique.

Examples of linear functions (More examples in Boyd book)

Negation $\( \mathbf{f}(\mathbf{x} ) = - \mathbf{x}, \)\( which can be written as \)\( \mathbf{f}(\mathbf{x} ) = (-\mathbf{I}) \mathbf{x} \)$

Reversal $\( \mathbf{f}(\mathbf{x} ) = \left[ x_{n-1}, x_{n-2}, \ldots, x_1, x_0 \right] \)$

Let $$ \mathbf{A} =

(14.4)#\[\begin{bmatrix} 0 & 0 & \ldots & 0 & 1 \\ 0 & 0 & \ldots & 1 & 0 \\ \vdots & \vdots & \vdots & \vdots \\ 1 & 0 & \ldots 0 & 0 \\ \end{bmatrix}\]

$\( Then \)\mathbf{f}(\mathbf{x}) = \mathbf{Ax}$

Examples of nonlinear functions

Quadratic function: each \(x_i \rightarrow x_{i}^2\)

Consider

(14.5)#\[\begin{align} \mathbf{f}(\mathbf{x}+\mathbf{y})&= \left[ (x_0+y_0)^2,~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~ (x_1+y_1)^2, ~~~~~~~~~~~~ \ldots \right] \\ &=\left[ x_{0}^{2} +2x_0 y_0+y_{0}^{2},~ ~ x_{1}^{2} +2x_1 y_1+y_{1}^{2},~~~ \ldots\right]\\ &\ne \mathbf{f}(\mathbf{x})+ \mathbf{f}(\mathbf{y}) \\ &= \left[ x_{0}^{2} +y_{0}^{2},~~~~~~~~~~~~ ~ x_{1}^{2} +y_{1}^{2},~~~ \ldots\right]\\ \end{align}\]

Absolute Value each \(x_i \rightarrow |x_i|\)

Consider \(n=1\)

(14.6)#\[\begin{align} f((-1)(1)+(0)(1))&= |-1| \\ &= 1 \\ &\ne (-1) f(1) + (0) f(1) \\ &= (-1) |1| + (0) |1| \\ &=-1 \end{align}\]

Sort consider the function \(\mathbf{f}(\mathbf{x})\) that sorts \(\mathbf{x}\) in decreasing order:

(14.7)#\[\begin{align} \mathbf{f}( [1,0]^T + [ 0,1]^T) &=\mathbf{f}( [1,1]^T) \\ &=[1,1]^T \\ & \ne \mathbf{f}([1,0]^T) + \mathbf{f}([ 0,1]^T) \\ &= [1,0]^T + [1,0]^T \\ &=[2,0]^T \end{align}\]

14.2.7.1. Affine Functions#

A vector-function is affine if \(\mathbf{f}(\mathbf{x}) = \mathbf{Ax} + \mathbf{b}\), where \(\mathbf{A}\) is a \(m \times n\) matrix and \(b\) is an \(m\)-vector

Affine functions are often casually referred to as linear, but they are not linear unless \(\mathbf{b} = \mathbf{0}\) because superposition does not hold in general

It can be shown that for affine functions, superposition holds in the special case that \(\alpha+\beta=1\):

(14.8)#\[\begin{align} \mathbf{f}(\alpha \mathbf{x} + \beta \mathbf{y}) &= \mathbf{A}(\alpha \mathbf{x} + \beta \mathbf{y}) +\mathbf{b} \\ &=\alpha \mathbf{A} \mathbf{x} + \beta \mathbf{A} \mathbf{y} +\mathbf{b} \\ &=\alpha \mathbf{f} (\mathbf{x}) + \beta \mathbf{f}(y) \\ &= \alpha \mathbf{A} \left(\mathbf{x} +\mathbf{b} \right) + \beta \left(\mathbf{A} \mathbf{y} +\mathbf{b} \right) \\ &= \alpha \mathbf{A} \mathbf{x} + \beta \mathbf{A} \mathbf{y} +(\alpha+\beta) \mathbf{b} \\ &= \alpha \mathbf{A} \mathbf{x} + \beta \mathbf{A} \mathbf{y} + \mathbf{b} \end{align}\]

14.2.8. Systems of Linear Equations#

One of the most important applications of linear vector functions is to solve linear systems of equations

Consider a system of \(m\) equations in \(n\) variables \(x_0, x_1, \ldots, x_{n-1}\):

(14.9)#\[\begin{align} A_{00}x_0 +A_{01}x_1 +···+A_{0,n-1}x_{n-1} &=b_0 \\ A_{10}x_0 +A_{11}x_1 +···+A_{0,n-1}x_{n-1} &=b_1 \\ \vdots\\ A_{m-1,0}x_0 +A_{m-1,1}x_1 +···+A_{m-1,n-1}x_{n-1} &=b_{m-1}, \\ \end{align}\]

where

  • \(A_{ij}\) are numbers and are called coefficients, and

  • \(b_j\) are numbers called right-hand sides

Then the system of linear equations can be written concisely in matrix notation as $\( \mathbf{Ax} = \mathbf{b}, \)\( where \)\mathbf{x}= [x_0, x_1, x_{n-1}]^T$ is the vector of variables (unknowns)

An \(n\)-vector of values \(\mathbf{x}\) is a solution to the system of linear equations if \(\mathbf{Ax}=\mathbf{b}\) holds

A system of linear equations may have:

  • no solutions

  • a unique solution

  • many solutions

Examples

Consider the set of equations

(14.10)#\[\begin{align} x_1 + x_2 &=1\\ x_1&=-1\\ x_1-x_2&=0\\ \end{align}\]

In matrix form, we would write $$

(14.11)#\[\begin{bmatrix} 1 & 1 \\ 1 & 0 \\ 1 & -1 \\ \end{bmatrix}\]
(14.12)#\[\begin{bmatrix} x_1\\ x_2\\ \end{bmatrix}\]

=

(14.13)#\[\begin{bmatrix} 1\\ -1\\ 0\\ \end{bmatrix}\]

$$

This system of equations has no solution:

  • If we use the first 2 equations, we have \(x_1=-1\) and \(x_2=2\)

  • If we use the second 2 equations, we have \(x_1=-1\) and \(x_2=-1\)

  • This set of equations is not consistent: they contradict eachother

Consider the set of equations

(14.14)#\[\begin{align} x_1 + x_2 &=1\\ x_2+x_3&=2\\ \end{align}\]

In matrix form, we would write $$

(14.15)#\[\begin{bmatrix} 1 & 1 & 0 \\ 0 & 1 & 1\\ \end{bmatrix}\]
(14.16)#\[\begin{bmatrix} x_1\\ x_2\\ x_3 \end{bmatrix}\]

=

(14.17)#\[\begin{bmatrix} 1\\ 2\\ \end{bmatrix}\]

$$

This system of equations has an infinite number of solutions. For instance \(\mathbf{x} = [1,0,2]^T\) and \(\mathbf{x} = [ 0, 1, 1]^T\)
