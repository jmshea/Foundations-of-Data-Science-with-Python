11.4. Chi-Square Goodness-of-Fit Test#

We can use different forms of contingency tables and chi-squared tests to determine whether some discrete numerical data come from a given distribution. In this section, we will show how to calculate the chi-squared test statistic given a model distribution and use this to calculate a \(p\)-value for the observed data given the model distribution. These types of tests are called chi-squared goodness-of-fit tests or one-way chi-square tests and exist in two basic varieties:

  • The first variety is the standard NHST model. We observe in the data some characteristic that makes us think that it cannot have come from some default distribution. The null hypothesis, \(H_0\) is that it came from the default distribution, and we estimate the probability of seeing such a large value of the chi-squared statistic conditioned on \(H_0\) being true.

  • In the second variety, we have a model for a distribution that the data may have come from, and we wish to determine whether the model is reasonable. We can still conduct a NHST, but now \(H_0\) is the hypothesis that the data came from the model distribution, and we conclude that the model is consistent with the data if the probability of seeing such a large value of the chi-squared statistic is large (typically much more than the usual threshold of \(0.05\)).

11.4.1. Standard NHST#

We start with an example that has been discussed in books and news stories for over a decade now. It appears that Major League Baseball Players are much more likely to have birthdays in August and the following months than in July and the months immediately previous to that. Some examples of articles and books discussing this follow.

  • I first came across this phenomenon while reading the Malcom Gladwell book Outliers: The Story of Success, Little, Brown and Company, 2008. Gladwell writes “more major league [baseball] players are born in August than in any other month”.

  • In a 2008 Slate article by Greg Spria entitled “The Boys of Late Summer”, the author states “Since 1950, a baby born in the United States in August has had a 50 percent to 60 percent better chance of making the big leagues than a baby born in July.” In addition, Sprira indicates that the book The Baseball Astrologer, published by Total/Sports Illustrated in 2000 makes the observation that “the sign under which an individual was born played a significant role in whether he made it in pro ball”.

Let’s look at the data and try to analyze it using what we know about contingency tables and chi-squared tests.

We now wish to determine whether the observed differences in the numbers of players born in different months is caused by some real effect or could just be explained by random variation in the data. We formulate this as a NHST in which \(H_0\) represents the scenario in which birth month does not influence whether a person plays MLB. I purposefully left the description of \(H_0\) vague because we are going to explore different models that are consistent with this description of \(H_0\). I want to show you that a statistician can take different levels of care in setting up a statistical test, and we will see how that affects the outcome of the test.

11.4.2. Testing Fit of a Model Distribution#

In the standard NHST test, we formulate a test to determine whether the data is sufficiently different from some baseline distribution that we can reject the null hypothesis, \(H_0\). In this section, we consider a similar approach, but instead of using some baseline distribution that the data suggests may be rejected, we are using a model distribution that we purposefully select to match the data.

Suppose we have some model for the distribution of the data, and we wish to assess how well the data fits the model. Typically, the model distribution is some standard discrete distribution, such as Binomial, Geometric, or Poisson. To assess the goodness of fit, the procedure is the standard NHST, except the distribution will be considered to be consistent with the data if the \(p\)-value is relatively large (much more than our significance threshold).

I will illustrate this with an example.

The Tropical Meteorology Project of the Department of Atmospheric Science at Colorado State University has a nice table that summarizes information about tropical storms in the North Atlantic Ocean by year at

We might guess that this data might follow a Poisson distribution for several reasons:

  • The region that contains the most probability might be considered as roughly bell shaped.

  • The presence of a few large values suggests that the data is not inherently constrained to some upper limit, like a Binomial random variable would be.

  • We might believe that storms occur “at random” at some rate, which would result in a Poisson distribution.

Recalling that the parameter \(\alpha\) of a Poisson distribution is the expected value of the random variable, we can estimate \(\alpha\) using the sample mean of the data:

alpha = named_storms.mean()
alpha

13.625

Let’s use SciPy’s stats.poisson() method to create a Poisson distribution with this value of \(\alpha\) and plot the expected number of instances for each number of storms, along with the actual number of instances:

# Generate the expected numbers of  years with specific numbers of storms

# Plot the observed data

plt.ylabel('Number of instances (years)');

# Plot the expected values

         linefmt='C1', markerfmt='x', label='Expected')

The Poisson model looks like it may be reasonable match, but there are some significant differences, especially for near the edges of the distribution, which corresponds to values that are more rare. This motivates us to conduct a one-way chi-squared test to evaluate the goodness of fit. We have two issues that we need to resolve first:

  1. The chi-squared approximation is only accurate if the expected number of occurrences in each cell is approximately 4 or larger, but we can see that many of the expected values are smaller than 4.

  2. I have only plotted the expected values from the reference distribution where the actual values are greater than 0, but the expected values from the reference distribution are nonzero for all integer numbers of storms. To overcome both these issues, we will aggregate the values at both edges of the distribution to achieve an expected value of at least 4. To do this, let’s group all values less than or equal to 10 into a cell, and all values greater than or equal to 16 into another cell.

Let’s start by copying the values and counts for the values from 10 to 16:

min_idx=np.where(storm_values==10)[0][0]

storm_counts2[0] = storm_counts[:min_idx+1].sum()

We now need to calculate the expected values for these cells from our Poisson model. We start by calculating the expected values without considering aggregation at the edges:

storm_expected = len(named_storms) * P.pmf(storm_values2)

And now we calculate the expected values at the left and right edge cell using the CDF and survival function, respectively, to get the probability of all of the aggregated cells:

storm_expected2 = storm_expected.copy()
storm_expected2[0] = len(named_storms) * P.cdf(storm_values2[0])
# Have to subtract 1 because the survival function is the probability of values strictly
# greater than its argument
storm_expected2[-1] = len(named_storms) * P.sf(storm_values2[-1]-1)
print('value | actual | expected')

The normalized squared errors are

normalized_storm_errors = storm_errors**2/storm_expected2

The test statistic is the sum of the normalized squared errors:

test_stat = normalized_storm_errors.sum()
test_stat

4.029457622896795

When we calculate the probability of seeing such a large-value of the test statistic, we have to reduce the number of degrees of freedom by the number of parameters that we estimated from the data. For this example, we estimated one parameter, the value of \(\alpha\) for the Poisson distribution. The normal number of degrees of freedom is the number of cells minus 1, so the adjusted number of degrees of freedom is the number of cells minus 2. We can calculate the probability of seeing such a large value of the test statistic using the survival function as shown below:

C = stats.chi2(len(storm_values2)-2)
C.sf(test_stat)

0.5451826192864491

Because this value is much larger than our typical significance threshold of 0.05, the Poisson model is consistent with this data. Note, however, that such a large \(p\)-value is not observed for all time ranges and aggregation methods. For instance, applying a similar analysis to the 80-year period from 1943 to 2022 results in a \(p\)-value of approximately 0.4, and the analysis for the 100-year period from 1923 to 2022 results in a \(p\)-value of approximately 0.2. Although these values are larger than our significance threshold and thus do not reject the Poisson model, the results suggest that the Poisson model may not be the best over these longer time periods. Some possible reasons for this are:

  • the rate at which storms occur is changing over time, for instance do to climate changes,

  • the criteria for naming storms is changing over time, or

  • the Poisson model is not the best model for this data.
